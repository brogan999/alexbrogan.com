General Thinking Concepts</h1><p id=""><strong id="">Modality effect</strong></p><p id="">You'll tend to remember more when using a mixed-mode (partly visual and partly auditory) of learning compared to a single-mode (either visual or auditory alone)</p><p id="">When information is presented in multiple modalities, total working memory capacity is increased</p><p id=""><strong id="">Next-in-line effect</strong></p><p id="">The tendency to have lower recall for events that happen right before or after a public performance‚Äîwhether performing on stage or talking to a group of other people</p><p id="">This effect is believed to occur due to both attention distraction and retrograde amnesia</p><p id=""><strong id="">Placement bias</strong></p><p id="">The tendency to be influenced by story placement in the news in a way that downplays information supportive of a minority viewpoint</p><p id="">As a general rule, story placement is a measure of how important the editor considers the story</p><p id=""><strong id="">Picture superiority effect</strong></p><p id="">The tendency for pictures and images to be remembered more easily than words</p><p id="">Occurs because our brains dually encode images, but encode words only once. This means that images are stored as pictures and words, but words are only stored as words</p><p id=""><strong id="">Positivity effect</strong></p><p id="">The tendency for elderly people to perceive and remember positive images and experiences more than negative ones</p><p id="">A form of selective memory that helps compensate for troubles that occur over the lifetime</p><p id=""><strong id="">Processing difficulty effect</strong></p><p id="">We have an easier time remembering information that takes longer to read and understand</p><p id="">This feels counter-intuitive: we want to make our message simple and short, right? In some cases, yes. But, not all. Making the brain work is a powerful tool</p><p id=""><strong id="">Rosy retrospection</strong></p><p id="">Our tendency to recall the past more fondly than the present</p><p id="">An inaccurate view of past events might lead to judging current or future events unfairly. And without storing negative experiences, you may fail to incorporate constructive feedback</p><p id=""><strong id="">Reminiscence bump</strong></p><p id="">The tendency for older adults (over forty) to have increased or enhanced recollection for events that occurred during their adolescence and early adulthood</p><p id="">Occurs because memory storage in autobiographical memory is not consistent through time</p><p id=""><strong id="">Self-relevance effect</strong></p><p id="">A tendency to encode information differently depending on whether we are implicated in the information</p><p id="">When we are asked to remember information when it is related in some way to ourselves, the recall rate can be improved</p><p id=""><strong id="">Source Confusion</strong></p><p id="">A type of memory error where we don't remember where certain memories come from</p><p id="">Dangerous because when we disassociate the content of our knowledge from the source, we become similarly confident in all content despite not being confident in all sources</p><p id=""><strong id="">Suffix effect</strong></p><p id="">The tendency to be unable to recall the final items from a spoken list when the list is followed by an unnecessary or irrelevant speech item or suffix</p><p id="">Occurs because the mind fixates on the very end of the list, even if it's not actually part of the list</p><p memory retention, and why repeated exposure over a longer period of time typically does</p><p id=""><strong id="">Telescoping effect</strong></p><p id="">Refers to inaccurate perceptions regarding time, where people see recent events as more remote than they are (backward telescoping), and remote events as more recent (forward telescoping)</p><p id="">Can occur whenever we make temporal assumptions regarding past events</p><p id=""><strong id="">Testing effect</strong></p><p id="">The tendency for long-term memory to be increased when some of the learning period is devoted to retrieving information from memory‚Äîin other words, devoted to 'active recall'</p><p id="">Flashcard software like Anki is an application of this</p><p id=""><strong id="">Tip-of-the-tongue through diet, exercise, socialisation, and games related to words such as scrabble</p><p id=""><strong id="">Verbatim effect</strong></p><p id="">People remember the gist of information, which is its general meaning, better than they remember its exact form</p><p id="">This has important implications for presenting information. We should preference storytelling over facts or statistics, they'll stick better</p><p id=""><strong id="">Von Restorff effect</strong></p><p id="">An item that ‚Äústands out like a sore thumb‚Äù is more likely to be remembered or preferenced than other items</p><p id="">Takeaway: Make important information visually distinctive</p><p id=""><strong id="">Subselves</strong></p><p id="">We use different mental processes in different situations, so each of us is not a single character but a collection of different characters, who take turns to commandeer the mind depending on the situation. There is a work ‚Äúyou‚Äù, a friend ‚Äúyou‚Äù, an online ‚Äúyou‚Äù, etc.</p><p id=""><strong id="">Nudging</strong></p><p id="">The tendency for positive reinforcement or indirect suggestions to influence behaviour and decision-making</p><p id="">A 'nudge' makes it more likely that an individual will make a particular choice, or behave in a particular way; be wary of what or who is nudging you</p><p id=""><strong id="">Clanning</strong></p><p id="">The tendency of humans group naturally around important issues, positions, or events</p><p id="">Understand the group dynamic, or you‚Äôre likely to be caught up in it</p><p id=""><strong id="">Meehl Pattern</strong></p><p id="">When an expert in a field tries to predict something complicated but has a lower success rate doing so than a simple statistical formula (or ‚Äòalgorithm‚Äô)</p><p id="">Sometimes experts overcomplicate explanations instead of relying on simple data (The Curse of Knowledge)</p><p id=""><strong id="">Thousands of You</strong></p><p id="">The tendency to assume that we're one person in everyone else's mind when in reality, a different version of us exists in the minds of everyone who knows us</p><p id="">The person you think of as "yourself" exists only for you, and even you don't really know who that is</p><p id=""><strong id="">Asch Negative</strong></p><p id="">A theory explaining social conformity</p><p id="">If someone is Asch positive, they follow the crowd over what they believe to be true</p><p id="">If someone is Asch negative, they follow what they believe to be true regardless of the crowd's opinion</p><p id=""><strong id="">Enantiodromia</strong></p><p id="">An excess of something can give rise to its opposite. Excess builds up tension, and the more extreme the position, the more easily it can shift to its opposite</p><p id="">E.g. A society that is too liberal will be tolerant of tyrants, who will eventually make it illiberal</p><p id=""><strong id="">Protect Yourself From Bad Advice</strong></p><p id="">People tend to be most motivated by incentives. Status or prestige? Responsibility? Freedom? like you know everyone else far better than they know you, and not only that, but you know them better than they know themselves</p><p id="">This illusion clouds your ability to see the people you disagree with as nuanced and complex</p><p id=""><strong id="">Enclothed Cognition</strong></p><p id="">The systematic influence that clothes have on our psychological processes</p><p id="">Clothing id="">Deindividuation</strong></p><p id="">A state when you become so immersed in the norms of the group that you lose your sense of identity and personal responsibility, which can ultimately lead to unsociable or "mob-like" behaviour</p><p id="">Anonymity and diffusion of responsibility act strongly here</p><p id=""><strong id="">Overjustification Effect</strong></p><p id="">A phenomenon in which getting paid for doing what you already enjoy will sometimes cause your love for the task to wane because you attribute your motivation as coming from the reward, not your internal feelings</p><p
